# Water Safety During Disasters

## Making Water Safe to Drink
If you are unsure whether your water is safe, treat it before drinking.

### Boiling
- Boiling is the surest method to kill disease-causing organisms.
- Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute (3 minutes above 6,500 feet elevation).
- Let it cool before drinking. Do not add ice unless the ice was made from safe water.
- Boiled water will taste flat. Pour it between two clean containers to add oxygen back.

### Chemical Disinfection
- If you cannot boil water, use unscented liquid household chlorine bleach (5-9% sodium hypochlorite).
- Add 8 drops (1/8 teaspoon) per gallon of clear water. If water is cloudy, use 16 drops.
- Stir and let stand for 30 minutes. Water should have a slight chlorine smell. If not, repeat the dose and wait 15 more minutes.
- Do not use bleach that has added cleaners or is color-safe.

### Water Filters
- Use filters that say "NSF 53" or "NSF 58" on the label. These remove most bacteria and parasites.
- Filters do NOT remove viruses. After filtering, disinfect with bleach or boiling if viral contamination is possible.

## Water Sources in Emergencies
- Store at least 1 gallon per person per day for at least 3 days.
- If supplies run out: rainwater, streams, and ponds can be treated. Avoid water with floating material, odor, or dark color.
- Use water from the hot water tank (turn off gas/electricity first, open the drain valve at the bottom).
- Use melted ice cubes from the freezer.
- Do NOT drink flood water, pool water (without treatment), or water from radiators, waterbeds, or toilets.

## Water Storage
- Store water in food-grade containers. Clean with a teaspoon of bleach per quart, then rinse.
- Replace stored water every 6 months.
- Store in a cool, dark place away from chemicals.
- Commercially bottled water in sealed containers is safe for years.

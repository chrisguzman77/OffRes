# Flood Safety Guide

## Before a Flood
- Know your area's flood risk. Check FEMA flood maps.
- Sign up for local emergency alerts.
- Prepare an emergency kit: water (1 gallon per person per day for 3 days), non-perishable food, flashlight, batteries, first aid kit, medications, important documents in a waterproof container.
- Know your evacuation routes. Plan multiple routes in case roads are blocked.
- Move important items to upper floors if you live in a flood-prone area.
- Consider flood insurance — standard homeowner insurance does not cover floods.

## During a Flood
- Move immediately to higher ground. Do not wait for instructions if you see rising water.
- Never walk, swim, or drive through flood waters. Turn Around, Don't Drown. Six inches of moving water can knock you down. One foot of water can float a vehicle.
- Stay off bridges over fast-moving water.
- If trapped in a building, go to the highest floor. Do not climb into a closed attic — you may become trapped by rising water. Go to the roof only if necessary, and signal for help.
- If trapped in a car, stay in the car if water is rising around it. If water is entering the car, climb onto the roof.
- Stay away from downed power lines and electrical wires. Electrocution is a major cause of death in floods.
- Do not touch flood water if you can avoid it. It may be contaminated with sewage, chemicals, and debris.

## After a Flood
- Return home only when authorities say it is safe.
- Avoid walking or driving through remaining flood water.
- Document damage with photographs for insurance claims.
- Clean and disinfect everything that got wet. Mud and standing water can contain sewage and chemicals.
- Watch for weakened structures — floors, walls, and foundations may be damaged.
- Discard any food that has been in contact with flood water.
- Be aware of the risk of mold. Dry out your home within 24-48 hours to prevent mold growth.

## Flood Water Dangers
- Flood water as shallow as 6 inches can knock an adult off their feet.
- Flood water at 2 feet can float a car.
- Flood water is often contaminated with raw sewage, industrial chemicals, gasoline, and agricultural waste.
- Standing in flood water can cause skin infections, especially if you have open wounds.
- Do not let children play in flood water.
